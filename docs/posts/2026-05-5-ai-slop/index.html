Why does AI Slop Make You So Angry?

A Cogntitive and Neurological Perspective
AI Images
AI Slop
Neuroscience
Author

Z Skigen, Jazelle Saligumba

Neuroscience @ Pomona College

Published

May 5, 2026

Ah. Another morning where, against my better judgement, I check my phone, open Instagram, and encounter a meme, or some sort of image. Whatever it is, it is almost certain I encounter something artificially generated; usually piss filtered1, cartoonish, hyperrealistic, yet imprecise.

As I created this project, I wanted to ground my research in an almost indescribable feeling that never fails to behold me every time I encounter an AI image. Words to describe such a feeling include: rage, discomfort, annoyance, and the main focus of this essay, a feeling of uncanny valley. Mori (1970) first described the uncanny valley in response to robots, the discomfort that emerges when something is almost-but-not-quite human. I believe AI images produce a similar effect. I wondered if anyone else shared this experience, and Reddit threads confirm I am not alone (Reddit user aiwars 2025).

Case Studies

Before explaining any of this, I want you to look at four images. They were all generated by AI. Notice what you feel, whatever it is, and remember it. The rest of this essay is an attempt to scientifically explain it.

Whatever emotion you felt in reaction to these images is the subject of this essay. The words people often think of when they encounter AI images tend to cluster around the same territory: wrongness, uncanniness, a sense that something is off without being able to say exactly what. This essay is an attempt to explain that feeling not as a neurological and cognitive event.

Fluency Theory and Predictive Processing

Looking at these images, I wanted to ground my discomfort in something more theoretical than personal opinion. There is a body of work in perceptual and cognitive science that explains what the brain is actually doing when it encounters a visual stimulus, and why some images feel wrong in ways that go beyond subjective preference. We can still hold the ethical critiques of AI image generation, the environmental cost of data centers, the exploitative nature of training data, while also arguing that the negative response to these images has a neurological basis that exists independently of those concerns.

Ramachandran and Hirstein (1999) propose eight laws of artistic experience, a set of heuristics that artists either consciously or unconsciously deploy to activate the visual areas of the brain. Three of these laws are especially relevant here: peak shift, perceptual grouping, and perceptual problem solving. What I want to show is that the images I am examining fail all three simultaneously, and that their failure is what generates the feeling I described at the start.

Peak Shift

The first law is peak shift. Consider the storefront image. At first glance it reads as a charming boutique, warm lighting, mannequins in coats with neatly folded merchandise. Looking closer, the sign above the window reads something like “HACKNAROO.” The image is vivid and heightened, clearly trying to evoke something, but it evokes nothing specific.

Ramachandran and Hirstein (1999) explain why through an animal learning experiment. If a rat is trained to distinguish a rectangle from a square and rewarded for picking the rectangle, it will respond even more strongly to a rectangle that is longer and skinnier than the original. The rat learned a rule, rectangularity, and more rectangle means better. Human visual processing works the same way. A caricature of Nixon is more viscerally Nixon than a photograph of Nixon because the cartoonist identified what makes his face distinct from all others and amplified those features. The result is what Ramachandran and Hirstein call a superstimulus, an image so precisely calibrated to the brain’s pattern-recognition rules that it produces a stronger neurological response than the real thing would. The brain asks “what makes this this?” and gets a satisfying answer.

The storefront does something that looks like peak shift but produces a different neurological outcome. AI images are trained on massive datasets and learn to reproduce whatever features appear most consistently across thousands of examples of a category, not what makes any particular instance of that category distinct. The storefront amplifies every feature associated with “charming boutique” in aggregate, the warm glow, the tasteful display, the brick exterior, without amplifying anything specific. The brain initiates the peak shift search, finds something that resembles a superstimulus, and then cannot locate the discriminative logic underneath it. There is nothing to find. That gap between what the image promises neurologically and what it delivers is where the discomfort begins, and as we will see with grouping and problem solving, it only compounds from there.

Perceptual Grouping

At first glance, the bar image reads as a busy, atmospheric bar, stools, bottles lined up behind the counter, warm light, people sitting and standing. These elements cohere locally with each one parsing. But the space they occupy is physically impossible. The ceiling is a submarine hatch. The corridor curves like a pressure vessel. The architecture belongs to an entirely different category of structure than the one being depicted. It is simultaneously a bar and a submarine interior, and no amount of looking resolves that contradiction.

Ramachandran and Hirstein’s (1999) third law explains why this produces a negative response. One of the brain’s primary jobs in vision is to find and delineate objects, to figure out what belongs together in the same physical world. When the brain successfully groups features into a coherent whole, that grouping is itself rewarding. It sends a signal to the limbic system before the object is even fully identified. This is why Ramachandran and Hirstein’s famous example works the way it does: a photograph that looks like random splotches suddenly resolves into a Dalmatian dog, and the moment those marks cohere into a recognizable shape, the viewer experiences a small but genuine sensation of pleasure, even mid-process. Coherence generates limbic activation in its own right. Composition in art is essentially the artist deliberately engineering that coherence so the brain can experience the reward of finding it.

What makes AI images particularly insidious under this framework is that they do not fail immediately. The brain starts its grouping work and gets small hits of reward, the face coheres, the scene reads as a bar, the general category is legible. Those local successes are the problem, because they invite the brain to keep going, to look for the global coherence that should follow. But it does not come. The lighting does not match the shadows. The background objects do not follow physical logic. The figures do not relate to the space they occupy in any way that holds together. The parts never add up to a whole.

An image that is completely incoherent from the start gets dismissed immediately. AI images are coherent enough to keep the brain engaged in the grouping search, but not coherent enough to complete it. The reward keeps getting promised and never arriving. That loop of partial coherence with no resolution is another layer of the same sustained discomfort the peak shift failure initiated. Unlike the failure we will see in the next law, there is no single detail to point to here. The incoherence is the space itself.

Perceptual Problem Solving

Look at the pickaxe image. The tool itself reads clearly enough, worn metal, wooden handle, a cave environment behind it. But stamped into the head of the pickaxe is the number 069, and below that an embossed symbol that suggests a manufacturer’s mark or serial designation. Your brain immediately asks why. Numbers on tools mean something. They are inventory markers, serial numbers, safety certifications. The brain initiates a search for the logic, what does 069 refer to, what system does this belong to? But it finds nothing. The number is there because the model produced it, not because it means anything. The embossed details do the same thing. They read as significant and resolve into nothing.

Ramachandran and Hirstein’s (1999) fifth law explains why this search feels compulsive. The brain finds pleasure in resolving ambiguity, the struggle toward a solution is itself rewarding, provided the solution eventually arrives. A veiled figure is more alluring than a fully visible one for this reason: partial concealment gives the brain something to work toward and then resolve. Neurologically, the reward is contingent on completion. The struggle is only pleasurable if it ends.

AI images structurally cannot satisfy this condition. The 069 stamp, the misaligned proportions, the details that suggest meaning without containing it all trigger the problem-solving mechanism and then offer no resolution. There is no underlying logic to discover because no one put any there. The brain does not shrug and move on. It keeps trying. A Picasso eventually resolves, the eyes on one side of the face start to make sense as a representational choice, the distortion coheres around an emotional logic, the brain gets its reward. AI errors offer no equivalent resolution because they are not choices. They are failures. The brain treats them as puzzles to be solved anyway. Stupid brain.

Peak shift promises essence and delivers average. Grouping promises coherence and delivers local fragments. Problem solving promises resolution and delivers nothing. The discomfort is the predictable output of perceptual systems running the same processes they always run, on material that structurally cannot satisfy them.

Fluency and Its Breakdown

Look at the bookstore man. The face registers immediately as a face. Your visual system has no trouble with that initial categorization, there is a person, they are sitting at a desk, there are bookshelves behind them. But something keeps not landing. The hair sits slightly wrong. The proportions of the face are almost right but not quite. The expression is legible as grumpy but the muscles producing it do not quite map onto how faces actually move. You keep looking and the image keeps almost cohering without ever fully doing so. Reber, Schwarz and Winkielman (2004) offer a precise account of why this produces a negative response. Their core claim is that aesthetic experience is a function of processing dynamics, the more fluently a perceiver can process an object, the more positive their response. Crucially this is not a conscious judgment. The brain registers ease of processing and that ease is itself experienced as pleasant before any opinion has formed. They call this hedonic marking, fluency just feels good, automatically. The variables that produce it are things like symmetry, figure-ground contrast, and goodness of form, features that make an image easy to parse.

What AI images produce is a specific split between two levels of this processing. Local fluency, the face reads as a face, the bookstore reads as a bookstore, the individual elements parse correctly, is intact. Global fluency, the way those elements cohere into a scene that makes spatial and physical sense, is absent. The processing starts smoothly, gets small positive signals from the local recognition, and then keeps hitting friction at the global level that never resolves. The brain is receiving conflicting information, fluency signals and disfluency signals simultaneously, and the result is a processing experience that promises ease and keeps withdrawing it.

Prediction Error Without Resolution

Return to the bookstore man. The face-processing system activates immediately and completely. The brain recognizes a human face and begins generating predictions with great specificity, not just “this is a face” but precise expectations about how the proportions should sit, how the hairline should meet the forehead, how the skin should move around the eyes. These are not casual expectations. The brain has spent a lifetime learning the statistical regularities of human faces and it applies that knowledge automatically and constantly. What happens next is that those predictions keep getting violated. The hairline is slightly wrong. The jaw does not sit right. The eyes are close but not quite. Each violation is small, but there are many of them, and none of them resolve.

Van de Cruys and Wagemans (2011) argue that this dynamic, prediction error followed by failed resolution, is precisely what generates sustained negative affect. Their framework, drawn from predictive coding models of visual perception, holds that the brain is fundamentally a prediction machine. At every level of visual processing it generates expectations about incoming stimuli, checks those expectations against what it actually receives, and flags the mismatches as prediction errors. Crucially, prediction errors are always initially negative. They signal that something in the brain’s model of the world needs updating.

What converts that initial negativity into pleasure is resolution. Van de Cruys and Wagemans (2011) are explicit that this is why a Picasso works despite being visually wrong. The errors are structured, intentional, and ultimately resolvable. The broken face coheres around an emotional logic once you find it. The brain does its work and gets its reward. The transition from uncertainty to predictability is what generates the positive aesthetic response. It is the solving of it that feels good, not the error itself. AI images cannot offer this. The bookstore man’s facial violations are artifacts of a model that has never seen a face, only patterns extracted from images of faces. There is no structure underneath to discover. The brain cycles through the prediction-error loop and comes up empty, then cycles again. The negative affect that prediction error generates never gets converted into resolution, it persists.

This is measurable. Van de Cruys and Wagemans (2011) note amygdala activation in response to unresolvable prediction errors, the same region involved in threat detection and fear processing. Reber, Schwarz and Winkielman’s (2004) psychophysiological work shows that disfluency produces activity in the corrugator muscle, the frowning muscle, before any conscious judgment has formed. The emotion is happening in your body before you have decided how you feel. The rage and discomfort you might notice scrolling past an AI image on Instagram are your threat-detection machinery responding to a genuine categorical violation, a face that pattern-matches to human but refuses to resolve into one.

Hard Isn’t the Same as Interesting

One obvious objection deserves an answer before we move on. Disfluency is not always unpleasant. Sometimes it is interesting. A difficult painting can be engaging. An ambiguous image can draw you in. If disfluency can generate positive responses, why does AI disfluency specifically generate negative ones?

Graf and Landwehr (2015) resolve this directly. Their research distinguishes between two routes to aesthetic liking: pleasure, which is driven by fluency and requires no effort, and interest, which is driven by the successful reduction of disfluency through controlled engagement. Interest is triggered by the movement from difficulty toward resolution. You engage, you work, the image opens up, and that progression is rewarding. AI images cannot offer this pathway. You can stare at the storefront sign as long as you want, bring as much focused attention to it as you can, and the disfluency will not reduce. HACKNAROO does not reveal itself as an acronym or a reference or a deliberate choice. The spatial impossibility of the bar does not resolve into a visual metaphor. The effort produces nothing. When Klimt’s sketch is ambiguous, sustained looking eventually reveals the figure. When Picasso’s face is broken, engagement eventually locates the emotional logic. The disfluency reduces. AI glitches do not reduce because they are failures with no underlying structure to discover. Without a reduction pathway, the disfluency cannot generate interest, and what remains is the sustained negative affect with no exit.

Aura Without Source

Look again at the bookstore man. He sits at a desk in a wood-paneled bookstore, a coffee cup in front of him, shelves of books behind him. He has the particular grumpiness of someone interrupted. He looks, in other words, like a person who was somewhere, doing something, when a photograph was taken. But he was not. There is no bookstore. There is no man. No one was there. Nothing was captured.

Walter Benjamin’s (1969) concept of aura helps articulate why this matters beyond the neurological failures already described. For Benjamin, aura is the quality of presence that attaches to an authentic work, its unique existence in a particular time and place, the history embedded in it, the fact that it happened. A photograph carries aura because it is indexical: it implies a there. Something stood in front of a lens. Someone decided to press a shutter. The image is evidence of an encounter between a camera and the world. Even a photograph of a mundane street corner, Benjamin writes, bears the trace of that moment, it is a scene of a crime, photographed to establish evidence of what was.

AI images have none of this. They are statistical distributions rendered visible, the average of millions of images collapsed into a single output. The bookstore man is not a portrait of anyone. The bar is not a place. The figures sitting at the counter in that impossible submarine interior were never anywhere. No photographer composed that shot. No one decided that the warm light should fall that way. The image has the surface of a document but no document exists. It is aura without source.

This is a different register of wrongness from what Ramachandran and Hirstein, Reber, Schwarz and Winkielman, Van de Cruys and Wagemans, and Graf and Landwehr describe. Those failures are perceptual and cognitive, the brain’s prediction machinery running loops it cannot exit. This failure is philosophical. It concerns what an image is allowed to claim about the world. A photograph of a bookstore claims that a bookstore existed. An AI image of a bookstore makes the same visual claim with nothing behind it. It occupies the visual register of evidence while being empty of it, a document of nothing, signed by no one.

But Why Do Some People Like AI Images?

If the negative reaction to AI images is neurological, why is it not universal? There are subreddits with tens of thousands of members celebrating AI art. There are markets for it, buyers for it, people who share it enthusiastically and feel nothing of what this essay describes. This deserves an honest answer rather than a dismissal.

Reber, Schwarz and Winkielman (2004) offer part of the explanation. Fluency effects are moderated by prior exposure, familiarity with a stimulus style builds processing fluency for that style specifically. People who consume large quantities of AI images develop a kind of literacy for the aesthetic. The piss filter, the hyper-saturated warmth, the uncanny smoothness of AI skin, these become recognizable and therefore easier to process. The mere exposure effect does not make the images neurologically coherent, but it makes them feel more familiar, and familiarity registers as fluency.

Context provides another part. Someone sharing an AI meme on Reddit is responding to its content, its humor, its category, and the neurological failure modes this essay describes are most acute when the brain is treating an image as a genuine representation, when it activates face-processing systems, grouping heuristics, and prediction machinery in earnest. Passive scrolling at speed may never push far enough into controlled processing for the global disfluency to become apparent.

This essay’s argument is strongest as a claim about a specific kind of encounter, sustained, attentive looking at an AI image treated as a depiction of something. Under those conditions, the failures described here are systematic and predictable. Whether that encounter is common probably depends on how you use these images and what you expect from them.

Human Sentiment on AI and the Uncanny Valley

In 1970, Masahiro Mori (1970) speculated that entities that appear as human-like will elicit feelings of uncanniness and eeriness to viewers. However, as those objects continue to reach even closer to levels of human-likeness, humans will once again achieve closer affinities to them again.

While Mori covered the uncanniness of physical objects, such as zombies and mechanical hands, the measure of the uncanny valley have since been applied to other mediums, such as large language models/chatbots, and generative AI images. Deepani Kishnani (2025) measured recent feelings of the 'uncanny valley' in two modes: through chatbots and AI images.

The first experiment entailed participants split to interact with three 'chatbots': a chatbot prompted to be 'uncanny-valley' like, a prompted near human-like chatbot, and a human control. The participants vastly preferred the human-like chatbot and human control over the uncanny-valley like chatbot. However, 60% of participants mistook the human control to be an AI chatbot, and 40% of participants mistook the AI chatbot to be human. This research points to the current human perception of AI and human responses and its blurred lines.

The second experiment studied participants responses to AI-generated images with varying degrees of prompts of 'uncanny valley', e.g. (hyperrealistic, semi-realistic, approaching uncanny valley.) Participants rated images in the middle ground, which were prompted to be intermediately realistic to be the most eery which supported the theory.

Complementing Kishnani's behavioral findings, Zhang et al. (2025)probed deeper to examine whether the bias against AI-labeled work is reflected not just in what people say, but in how their brains respond. In a study of 125 participants, identical artworks were presented under two labels: human-made or AI-made.

The neural data told a striking story. EEG recordings revealed higher P300 amplitudes and greater alpha power when participants evaluated human-labeled paintings, signatures of heightened attentional engagement and reflective processing. fNIRS data reinforced this, showing increased activation in the right angular gyrus and stronger functional connectivity between the inferior frontal gyrus and angular gyrus for human-labeled works — regions associated with deeper semantic interpretation and emotional resonance. In other words, participants' brains were simply doing more when they believed a human was behind the canvas.

Behaviorally, the pattern held: participants rated human-labeled artworks as more creative and delicate, and spent measurably more time evaluating them. This preference, however, was not grounded in any objective difference in the work itself — only in its attributed origin.

Yet if label alone can shape neural and aesthetic response, what happens when the visual itself is the source of unease? MacDorman et al. (2014) complicated the intuitive assumption that photorealism is always the safer creative choice. Studying computer-generated facial animations, they found that peak photorealism did not produce peak eeriness but rather, it was obviously artificial faces that participants found most unsettling. The uncanny valley, it turns out, is not simply a function of how close something gets to human likeness, but of how inconsistently it lands there. MacDorman et al. suggest that creators working with CG faces should either deliberately avoid full photorealism, or, if realism is the goal, ensure the result adheres closely to naturalistic aesthetic norms — faces that sit comfortably within the range of what we expect a human face to look like. Straddling that middle ground, where something is almost human but visibly not, is precisely where discomfort takes hold.

Taken together, Kishnani (2025) and Zhang et al. (2025) paint a consistent picture: human sentiment toward AI is shaped less by what AI actually produces and more by the mental frameworks people bring to the encounter. Whether navigating a conversation or contemplating a painting, the knowledge or belief that a human is on the other side activates something qualitatively different in us. As generative AI grows increasingly indistinguishable from human output, these findings raise an uncomfortable question: are we responding to the work, or merely to the story we tell ourselves about who made it?

Conclusion

The frameworks assembled here, Ramachandran and Hirstein (1999), Reber, Schwarz and Winkielman (2004), Van de Cruys and Wagemans (2011), Graf and Landwehr (2015), Benjamin (1969), all point toward the same structural conclusion: the negative reaction to AI images is the predictable output of perceptual and cognitive systems encountering images that promise coherence and repeatedly withhold it, documents that claim presence while containing none.

But this raises a question that none of these frameworks fully answers. If the failures described here are technical, the wrong number of fingers, the impossible architecture, the nonsense sign, then they are in principle fixable. Models are already improving. The hands are getting better. The spaces are becoming more physically coherent. Helliwell (forthcoming), writing on the aesthetics of AI art, notes precisely this: as AI systems improve, the distinctive weirdness of their outputs diminishes. The norm violations become subtler, the uncanny valley narrows, the failure becomes harder to locate.

So the open question is whether fixing the technical failures fixes the problem this essay describes. If the bar image had consistent architecture, if the bookstore man’s face resolved properly, if the pickaxe stamp meant something, would the negative reaction disappear? The neurological and cognitive frameworks suggest it might. The local and global fluency would align, the prediction errors would resolve, the grouping would cohere. The brain would get its rewards.

But Benjamin suggests otherwise. Even a technically perfect AI image would remain a document of nothing, signed by no one. The aura problem is a rendering problem in reverse, the better the rendering, the more insistently the image claims a presence it does not have. The bookstore man could have perfect proportions and still never have existed, still have been captured by no camera, still occupy a space that was never anywhere. Whether that absence is legible to a viewer who does not know to look for it is a different question. Whether it matters aesthetically if it is not legible is a different question still. These are the questions that will become more pressing as the technical failures recede. For now, the seven-fingered hand makes them easy to avoid.
